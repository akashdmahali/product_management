01 — The whole thing on one screen

The five layers of a telco

Every telecom operator — whether it's a giant like Airtel or a tiny MVNO reselling SIMs — is built from the same five stacked layers. Money and intent flow down; service and data flow up. Tap any layer to jump straight to it.

Explorer · the simple version

Imagine a telco as a restaurant. The Network is the kitchen and ovens — it cooks the actual food (the phone calls, the data). OSS is the kitchen manager making sure every station works and the dish gets made correctly. BSS is the front-of-house: the menu, taking your order, the bill, the loyalty card. Customers are the diners. And the Supply Chain is everyone who sells the restaurant its ovens, ingredients and the licence to operate. A telco lives or dies on how smoothly these five hand off to each other.

Architect · the precise version

The canonical split is BSS (the commercial domain) and OSS (the operational/technical domain), both sitting on top of the network. The clean mental model: a customer's intent enters at BSS (a product order), is decomposed into service and resource orders by OSS, and is realised as configuration on physical/virtual network functions. Usage then flows back up — events are mediated, rated, charged and billed. TM Forum's eTOM formalises this as three vertical end-to-end flows you'll meet everywhere: Fulfillment, Assurance, Billing (the "FAB" columns). Master FAB and you can place almost any telco capability on the map.

The one sentence to remember: BSS is "how the telco makes money"; OSS is "how the telco keeps the lights on"; the network is "what they're actually selling." Everything else is detail.

02 — Who's in the room

The players & the value chain

"A telco" is not one kind of company. There's a whole ecosystem of operators, resellers and middlemen — and knowing who is who is half of sounding like an insider.

Who you sell to: the three customer segments

◉
Retail / Consumer

B2C

Individual mobile, SIM, data and broadband users. Huge volume, low margin, brutal churn. Mostly prepaid in India; postpaid-heavy in the US/EU.

◫
SMB

Small & Medium Business

Group SIM fleets, call-centre lines, IoT SIMs (trackers, POS, meters). Higher value per account, lighter touch than enterprise.

▤
Enterprise / Wholesale

B2B & B2B2X

Leased data lines, MPLS/SD-WAN, private 5G, dedicated SLAs. Lower volume, premium margin, contract-driven, integration-heavy.

+ How international roaming actually works zoom in

When your SIM lands in another country, your home operator (the HPMN — Home Public Mobile Network) has no kit there. Your phone attaches to a VPMN (Visited PMN). The two need to (a) verify you're a real, paying subscriber and (b) agree who pays whom. That trust + billing relationship runs over the IPX backbone using signalling (legacy SS7/Diameter, now increasingly 5G's HTTP/2-based interfaces). Usage records (TAP/RAP files) are exchanged and reconciled between operators — this is inter-operator settlement, a whole BSS discipline of its own.

Your phone is like a member of a gym chain. When you travel, you walk into a different gym (a local network). That gym lets you in, but later sends a bill to your home gym for the time you used. The "billing club" that lets all these gyms trust each other and settle up is the roaming/IPX system.

🇮🇳 India angle

India is a massive inbound & outbound roaming market and a hub for IPX/wholesale via players like Tata Communications — which also owns a large slice of the world's subsea cable capacity.

Why this matters for building a telco: you don't have to be an MNO to start. Many modern "telcos" launch as an MVNO on an MVNE platform — renting network and software so they can focus on brand, segment and customer experience. That choice cascades into every layer above.

The network, briefly

Each "G" is just a bigger, faster pipe with new tricks. 2G could carry your voice and a text. 5G can carry a self-driving car's reflexes and a million smart meters and your 4K movie — all on the same tower, sliced into separate virtual lanes.

+ RAN, Core, Transport & the vendor map zoom in
  • RAN (Radio Access Network): the antennas + radios on towers that talk to your phone. The single most expensive, vendor-locked part of the network. Dominated by Ericsson, Nokia, Huawei, Samsung.
  • Core network: the "brain" — authenticates the SIM, applies policy, routes traffic, and (crucially) generates the usage events that BSS turns into money.
  • Transport / backhaul: fibre, microwave and MPLS links carrying traffic from tower to core.
  • Subsea & long-haul: the cables connecting continents — owned by carriers (Tata) and increasingly hyperscalers (Google, Meta).
  • Open RAN: the industry's attempt to break vendor lock-in by standardising interfaces so you can mix-and-match radios and software. Promising, politically loved, but adoption is slower and messier than hoped — see Problems.
🇮🇳 India angle

India ran one of the world's largest nationwide 5G Standalone (SA) rollouts — Jio and Airtel moved fast. Spectrum was auctioned in 2022 & 2024 across sub-1GHz to mmWave. India also pushes indigenous telecom gear (e.g. domestic 4G/5G stacks) as a supply-chain & sovereignty play.

04 — The commercial brain · the deep dive

BSS — where signals
become money

This is the layer you most want command over. BSS is the suite of systems that runs the commercial life of the telco: define a product, sell it, take the order, measure usage, price it, bill it, collect the cash, settle with partners, and keep the customer. Here's the full machine, component by component.

Explorer · the simple version

BSS is the shop counter, the cash register, the menu, the receipt and the loyalty programme — all in software. When you buy a plan, top up, get a bill, complain, or switch deals, you're touching BSS. It never touches a single radio wave; its whole job is turning "a customer used the network" into "the customer pays the right amount, on time, and stays happy."

Architect · the precise version

BSS is the realisation of eTOM's customer-facing processes — primarily the Fulfillment and Billing columns plus the customer side of Assurance. Historically delivered as monolithic, deeply customised suites (Amdocs, Ericsson, Netcracker, CSG, Oracle BRM). The modern direction is modular, cloud-native, API-first components wired by TM Forum Open APIs over a shared data model (SID) — see Frameworks. The strategic battleground today is the convergent charging system and a single, real-time product catalog driving everything downstream.

The BSS order-to-cash flow

A product order's journey through BSS (and the handoff to OSS)

The BSS components, one by one

Tap each to zoom in. In a monolith these blur together; in a modern modular BSS each is a separately deployable component with its own Open APIs.

+ 1 · Product Catalog & Offer Management the master source of truth

The catalog defines everything that can be sold: products, offers, bundles, pricing, eligibility rules, discounts and their relationships. Modern best practice is a single, centralized, model-driven catalog that drives CRM, ordering, charging and billing — so launching a new plan is a configuration change, not a 6-month IT project. This is the #1 lever for time-to-market, which is the metric CFOs care about.

It's the menu. If the menu is one clean, shared document, every waiter quotes the same price and a new dish appears everywhere instantly. If every waiter keeps their own copy of the menu (the old monolith problem), chaos and wrong bills follow.

  • Why it's strategic: a good catalog = launch a 5G IoT bundle in days; a bad one = months of custom code.
  • TM Forum link: Product Catalog APIs (TMF620), and the SID's Product/Service/Resource model.
+ 2 · CRM & Customer Management the relationship

Captures the party (person/organisation), identity & KYC, the account hierarchy (vital for SMB/enterprise multi-line), interactions across all channels (app, store, IVR, web), cases/tickets and the 360° view. Feeds churn models and personalization. In ODA this maps to Party Management + Engagement Management.

It's the little black book of who your customers are, what they've bought, every time they called, and how happy they are — so you can keep them.

🇮🇳 India angle

KYC is heavy and regulated — Aadhaar-based eKYC dramatically sped up SIM activation, but identity/verification rules (and re-verification mandates) are a real compliance load on CRM/onboarding.

+ 3 · Order Management (the orchestrator) turns a sale into action

Takes a commercial order from CRM and orchestrates it into a sequence of service and resource orders, coordinating BSS↔OSS. It manages decomposition, dependency sequencing, rollback on failure, and order state across long-running fulfilment. This is the classic BSS/OSS boundary — and where most integration pain lives. Open API: TMF622 (Product Ordering) handing to TMF641 (Service Ordering).

It's the manager who hears "one large pizza, half pepperoni" and turns it into precise instructions for the dough station, the oven and the delivery driver — and notices if any step fails.

+ 4 · Mediation the usage funnel

The network spews enormous volumes of raw usage events — CDRs (Call Detail Records) and their data/IP equivalents — in many proprietary formats. Mediation collects, normalizes, de-duplicates, correlates and enriches these into clean records the charging/billing engines can use. It's unglamorous and absolutely critical: garbage in, wrong bills out, revenue leaked.

Imagine every machine in a factory shouting its activity in a different language. Mediation is the translator that turns all that noise into one tidy logbook everyone can read.

+ 5 · Rating & Charging — online vs offline (the crown jewel) most strategic

Rating applies price to usage (and to events). Charging decides when and against what balance:

  • Offline charging: usage is rated and billed after the fact — the classic postpaid model. Records flow via mediation to billing at cycle end.
  • Online charging (OCS — Online Charging System): real-time balance checks before/while the service is used — the prepaid model. The OCS authorizes, reserves and debits credit live, so a phone cuts off when the balance hits zero.
  • Convergent charging: one engine handling prepaid + postpaid + any service (voice, data, IoT, content) against shared balances. This is the modern target architecture — and the part vendors compete hardest on for 5G.

Postpaid is a bar tab — drink now, settle later. Prepaid is a pay-as-you-go arcade card — the machine checks your credit before each game and stops you the instant it runs out. The "online charging system" is the lightning-fast cashier doing that check in real time, millions of times a second.

Why this is the crown jewel: in prepaid-heavy markets (India, much of Africa/Asia/LatAm) the OCS is the beating heart of the business — it must be real-time, never wrong, and never down. 5G slicing & B2B2X make charging models exponentially more complex, which is exactly why charging dominates modern BSS roadmaps.

+ 6 · Billing & Invoicing producing the bill

Aggregates rated usage + recurring charges + one-offs + discounts + taxes into an invoice per billing cycle, across complex account hierarchies (think an enterprise with 10,000 lines and a custom discount). Handles bill runs, adjustments, disputes, dunning and credit notes. Distinct from charging: charging knows "this call cost ₹2"; billing assembles the monthly statement and chases payment.

It's the part that gathers everything you did this month, adds it up, applies your discounts and taxes, and produces the actual bill you receive.

+ 7 · Revenue Management — assurance, settlement & fraud protect the cash
  • Revenue Assurance (RA): detect & plug revenue leakage — usage that happened but never got billed (mis-rated, dropped records, config errors). Operators routinely leak 1–5%+ of revenue here; RA is pure margin recovery.
  • Partner / interconnect settlement: reconcile who-owes-whom with other carriers, roaming partners, content providers and the whole B2B2X ecosystem. Increasingly the biggest growth area as telcos become platforms.
  • Fraud Management: SIM-box fraud, subscription fraud, roaming fraud, Wangiri scams. Real-time detection sits alongside charging.

This is the loss-prevention team: making sure money the telco earned doesn't quietly slip through cracks, that partners pay their share, and that crooks don't ride for free.

The monolith problem (your starting premise, made precise): classic BSS suites (Amdocs & co.) bundled all seven blocks into one deeply-customized, tightly-coupled platform. Powerful, but slow to change, expensive to upgrade, and a barrier to launching anything new fast. That pain is the reason the whole industry is moving to modular, API-driven BSS — which is what the TM Forum frameworks exist to enable.

05 — The technical nervous system

OSS — keeping the lights on

If BSS is the shop, OSS is the engine room. It's how an order becomes a live service, how the network is tracked, and how problems get caught before customers scream. You asked for the basics — here's a clean, sufficient model.

Explorer · the simple version

OSS is the building's facilities & maintenance crew. It knows where every cable and box is (inventory), switches your new service on (provisioning), and watches for anything breaking 24/7 (assurance) — fixing many faults before you even notice.

The three jobs of OSS — and the FAB columns

Fulfillment

Make it work

Provisioning & activation: configure the network so the service the customer ordered actually exists and is live. Includes resource & service inventory.

Assurance

Keep it working

Fault management (detect/diagnose/fix outages) + performance management (monitor quality, SLAs, capacity). The 24/7 watchtower.

Inventory

Know what you have

The single source of truth for physical & logical network resources — towers, ports, IP addresses, fibre, spectrum, VNFs. Everything else depends on it.

The framework hook: eTOM organizes both BSS and OSS work into three end-to-end vertical flows — Fulfillment, Assurance, Billing (FAB). BSS owns the customer-facing slice of each; OSS owns the network-facing slice. Knowing which side of FAB you're on instantly tells you whether a capability is "BSS" or "OSS."

+ The BSS ↔ OSS handoff, precisely zoom in

A product order (BSS) is decomposed into a service order (OSS service layer) and then resource orders (OSS resource layer), which become device/VNF configuration. This is the SID's Product → Service → Resource abstraction made operational. The trend (ODA's "decoupling") is to deliberately break the tight coupling between the commercial offer and the network realization, so product innovation and network operations can move at different speeds. Network-as-a-Service is the poster child.

BSS says "the customer wants Plan X." OSS translates that into "switch on these specific cables and boxes." Keeping those two languages loosely connected means the marketing team can invent new plans without waiting for the network team to rewire anything.

06 — The blueprints everyone agrees on

The TM Forum frameworks & ODA

This is the part that turns "I know telecom" into "I can architect a telecom." TM Forum is the industry body whose frameworks give every operator and vendor a common language — so systems, partners and people interoperate instead of reinventing the wheel. Learn these four and the modern architecture (ODA) and you genuinely have the toolkit to design a telco.

+ eTOM, decoded — the map you must internalize the big one

eTOM (enhanced Telecom Operations Map) is a hierarchical catalogue of every business process a service provider runs, decomposed from a few high-level areas down into granular tasks (current release v25.0, Nov 2025). It is a reference framework — descriptive, not prescriptive: it tells you what exists and how it relates, not a mandated sequence. You use it to scope projects, find gaps/duplication, compare As-Is vs To-Be, and speak a common language with vendors.

eTOM is a giant, agreed-upon list of everything a phone company does — sorted into neat drawers — so that when two companies (or a company and a software vendor) talk, they mean the same thing by "billing" or "assurance."

eTOM at a glance — 3 areas, and the famous FAB columns inside Operations

The mental shortcut: three areas — build it (SIP), run it (Operations), manage the company (Enterprise). Inside "run it," everything is Fulfillment, Assurance or Billing. That's 80% of the practical value of eTOM in two sentences.

+ SID, Functional Framework & Open APIs the other three
  • SID (Information Framework): the shared data dictionary. Its spine is the Product → Service → Resource hierarchy: what the customer buys (Product), the technical capability behind it (Service), and the physical/logical assets that deliver it (Resource). If everyone models data the SID way, systems integrate cleanly.
  • Functional Framework (the evolution of the old TAM / Application Framework): defines the functions software must perform, shifting from "which applications do I own" to "which capabilities do I need" — the conceptual basis for ODA components.
  • Open APIs: 100+ standardized REST APIs (TMF6xx series) that let modular components plug together regardless of vendor. They're the practical glue that makes "swap out one component" actually possible. The newer CAMARA / Open Gateway initiative extends this idea to expose network capabilities (quality-on-demand, location, SIM-swap fraud checks) to outside developers — a fresh revenue frontier.

ODA — where it's all heading (the "how")

The four frameworks describe the pieces. Open Digital Architecture (ODA) is TM Forum's blueprint for assembling them into a cloud-native, modular, plug-and-play operator — the explicit antidote to the monolith.

The idea

Lego, not concrete

Replace monolithic BSS/OSS with reusable, interoperable ODA Components — software building blocks that expose their function via Open APIs and run on a standard cloud-native substrate called the ODA Canvas. Assemble, disassemble and reassemble to meet needs. Vendors can now certify components against TM Forum's test kits.

Instead of buying one giant frozen machine that's painful to change, you build your telco from standard Lego bricks that snap together — and you can swap any brick without rebuilding the whole thing.

The structure

Functional blocks

ODA groups capability into functional blocks — e.g. Engagement Management (channels), Party Management (people/orgs), Core Commerce Management (catalog, order, billing), Production (service/resource lifecycle, supply chain, workforce), and a Decoupling & Integration fabric plus an Intelligence layer for real-time, zero-touch decisions.

ODA sorts all the Lego bricks into labelled bins: "talking to customers," "knowing who people are," "selling & billing," "making the service happen," and "the smart glue connecting it all."

How they fit together (say this in an interview and you'll sound senior): "eTOM gives me the processes, SID gives me the data model, the Functional Framework gives me the capabilities, Open APIs give me the interfaces — and ODA is the cloud-native architecture that assembles all four into composable components on a common canvas. It's how you migrate off the monolith without a big-bang rewrite."

07 — Why this is a hard business

The forces squeezing telecom

To have command, you must know what keeps telco executives awake. These pressures explain every strategic move the industry is making — and they're sharper than ever in 2026.

Economics

The ARPU trap

Data traffic explodes; revenue doesn't. Connectivity is commoditized, ARPU is flat or falling (often below inflation), and price wars in saturated markets drag everyone down. "More data for the same money" is the core squeeze.

Capital

Capex vs. returns

Operators sank fortunes into 5G & fibre; returns lag badly. With buildouts maturing, capex is now tapering in most regions — the new game is squeezing efficiency & monetization from what's already built, not building more.

5G monetization

The "underwhelming chapter"

5G's killer B2C app never quite arrived; consumers won't pay more for it. The money must come from enterprise, slicing, private networks & APIs — but that needs different sales models and modern BSS, which most operators are still building.

Tech debt

Legacy & architectural debt

Decades of monolithic, customized BSS/OSS. You can't get full value from AI or new services until you fix data governance and architecture — but modernization is unglamorous and competes for the same budget as AI. The defining tension of 2026.

Regulation

Rules & spectrum

Spectrum is scarce, expensive and government-controlled. Add data privacy, lawful intercept, net-neutrality, satellite/OTT debates, security mandates and consumer protection. Regulators shape the market structure itself.

Geopolitics

Supply-chain & security

The RAN market is dangerously concentrated (Ericsson, Nokia, Huawei, Samsung). Banning Huawei in many markets left some with only two real choices. Open RAN promised diversification but has under-delivered. Vendor revenue is shrinking — a fragile supply base.

Competition

Churn & new entrants

Number portability makes switching trivial; OTT players (WhatsApp, etc.) ate voice/SMS revenue; hyperscalers and satellite (Starlink) now circle. Defending the customer relationship is existential.

Fraud & leakage

Money slipping away

Revenue leakage, SIM-box fraud, roaming fraud, interconnect disputes. At telco scale, a 1% leak is enormous — which is why revenue assurance & fraud sit right beside charging.

+ Supply chain & Open RAN — the deeper story zoom in

The telecom equipment market contracted sharply (an ~11% drop in 2024, the worst in 20+ years) as operators cut capex post-5G-peak. Western policy banned Huawei to favour alternatives, but instead of consolidating around secure vendors, the market fragmented, leaving Huawei-free countries reliant on essentially two RAN suppliers. Open RAN — disaggregating RAN into interoperable, multi-vendor components — was meant to fix this; governments funded it (UK, US). But brownfield operators value reliability and single-vendor accountability, so adoption lags and many "Open RAN" deals are effectively single-vendor. Net effect: a concentrated, financially-stressed, geopolitically-exposed supply base — a genuine resilience risk.

There are only a handful of companies in the world that can build the radios on cell towers. Politics ruled some of them out, leaving very few choices. An effort called "Open RAN" tried to let companies mix-and-match parts from many suppliers (like building a PC), but it's been slower and harder than hoped — so the world still leans on a tiny, fragile set of suppliers.

🇮🇳 India angle — the regulatory & competitive landscape

The Telecommunications Act, 2023 replaced the 138-year-old Telegraph Act (1885) — moving from a licensing to an authorisation model, renaming the USO Fund as Digital Bharat Nidhi, and explicitly pulling satellite, M2M and cybersecurity into scope. The market has consolidated to essentially Jio, Airtel & (a struggling) Vi + BSNL — raising real questions about whether competition keeps prices fair. Live battlegrounds: the long-running AGR (Adjusted Gross Revenue) liabilities saga (with targeted relief for Vi given govt equity), satellite-spectrum pricing (Starlink/OneWeb vs. terrestrial players over a "level playing field"), and spectrum auctions across sub-1GHz → mmWave. TRAI recommends; DoT decides — and the two don't always agree.

08 — The direction of travel

Where the industry is moving

Every trend below is a direct response to the forces in section 07. If you can connect each solution back to the problem it solves, you're thinking like a telco strategist.

Architecture

Monolith → modular, cloud-native BSS/OSS

The big one: replace Amdocs-style suites with composable ODA components wired by Open APIs, running on cloud/Kubernetes. Goal: launch products in days, swap vendors, scale elastically. solves: tech debt + time-to-market

Intelligence

AI-native & autonomous networks

From AI pilots to AI as the operating system of the telco: self-healing networks, predictive assurance, automated charging/care, "zero-touch" operations. Also the inference/edge-compute opportunity. solves: opex + 5G monetization

Business model

TechCo · NetCo / ServCo / InfraCo

Structural reinvention: become a platform ("TechCo"), or delayer — split the network (NetCo/InfraCo, asset-heavy) from the customer business (ServCo, asset-light) to optimize capital. Plus consolidation & fixed-mobile-satellite convergence to lock in ARPU. solves: capex returns

Revenue

Network-as-a-Service & API monetization

Sell capabilities, not just bandwidth: network slices, private 5G, and exposing network functions to developers via CAMARA / Open Gateway APIs (quality-on-demand, location, anti-fraud). The hunt for the elusive new revenue line. solves: ARPU + enterprise growth

Access

eSIM / iSIM & 5G SA

Programmable SIMs cut activation friction and reshape onboarding/CRM. 5G Standalone unlocks slicing & deterministic SLAs — the technical prerequisite for premium B2B charging. solves: friction + 5G value

Edge & beyond

Edge, sovereign AI & subsea for AI

Telcos repositioning as the foundational connectivity & data-transport layer for AI — hosting inference at the edge, selling AI-era backhaul/DCI, and (where they own subsea/fibre) carrying the east-west AI traffic boom. solves: capex monetization

The throughline: connectivity is commoditized, so value migrates up (software, platforms, AI, enterprise outcomes) and the cost base must come down (cloud-native, automation, vendor flexibility). BSS modernization sits at the exact center of both moves — which is why it's where the money and the careers are.

09 — The capstone

How to actually build a telco

You said the real goal: after learning eTOM, help someone build a telecom company. Here's the end-to-end playbook, using everything above. Each step names the frameworks & components you'd reach for.

The greenfield operator playbook

  1. Pick your model & segment. MNO (own spectrum + network — huge capex, regulatory) or MVNO on an MVNE (asset-light, fast)? Retail, SMB, IoT, or enterprise? This single choice cascades through every layer. force: capex vs. speed
  2. Secure the rights. Authorisation/licence + spectrum (or a wholesale/MVNO agreement). In India: authorisation under the Telecom Act 2023, dealing with DoT. regulation
  3. Map your business with eTOM. Define which Level-1/2 processes you'll run across SIP, Operations (FAB) and Enterprise Management. This becomes your scope, your org chart, and your RFP language. eTOM
  4. Model your world with SID. Define your Product → Service → Resource catalog data model so every system speaks the same language from day one. SID
  5. Choose an architecture: go modular. Design a cloud-native, ODA-aligned stack of components on an ODA Canvas — don't buy a monolith you'll regret. ODA
  6. Stand up BSS. Catalog → CRM → Order Management → Mediation → Convergent Charging (OCS for prepaid) → Billing → Revenue Management. Wire them with Open APIs. Decide prepaid/postpaid/convergent early. BSS
  7. Stand up OSS. Inventory (source of truth) → Fulfillment/provisioning → Assurance (fault + performance). Define the BSS↔OSS order-decomposition handoff. OSS
  8. Source the network & supply chain. RAN/core vendors (or RAN-sharing), transport, interconnect/IPX for roaming, subsea/wholesale capacity. Plan for vendor concentration risk. supply chain
  9. Plan monetization & partners. Pricing, partner settlement, B2B2X, and expose capabilities via CAMARA/Open Gateway APIs. Build revenue assurance & fraud in from the start, not after you leak. revenue
  10. Operate, assure, evolve. Layer in AI/automation for zero-touch ops, watch ARPU/churn obsessively, and keep the architecture composable so you can adapt as 5G→6G and regulation shift. AI-native

If you can walk someone through those ten steps — naming the eTOM processes, the SID model, the BSS/OSS components and the ODA assembly at each one — you have exactly the command you set out to build. The rest is depth, which the zoom-ins above (and the PDF companion) give you on demand.

eTOM Level-2 — the real grid

On the map, eTOM was three big areas and the FAB columns. That's Level 1. To actually scope a project or read a vendor RFP, you work one layer down — Level 2, the named process blocks. This is that layer, laid out as the grid every practitioner carries in their head.

Explorer · the simple version

Think of a hospital. Level 1 is "the hospital." Level 2 is the named departments — A&E, surgery, pharmacy, billing. You don't manage "a hospital"; you manage departments, and you trace a patient through them. eTOM Level-2 is the telco's named departments, and a customer order is the patient moving through them.

Architect · the precise version

eTOM is a strict decomposition: Level 0 (enterprise) → Level 1 (process groupings) → Level 2 (the process blocks you scope around) → Level 3 (detailed sub-processes) → Level 4 (tasks). Level 2 is the Goldilocks layer — granular enough to be real capabilities you can own, staff and procure; coarse enough to fit on one page. In the Operations area, Level-2 processes sit at the intersection of a functional row (CRM, SM&O, RM&O, S/PRM) and a FAB column.

The payoff: trace one order across the grid

The grid isn't a filing cabinet — it's a circuit board. Real value comes from following an end-to-end flow as it hands off between Level-2 processes, row by row, down the stack:

▸ FULFILLMENT flow (order → live service)

Selling → Order Handling (CRM) → Service Configuration & Activation (SM&O) → Resource Provisioning (RM&O) → S/P Requisition Mgmt (S/PRM, if a partner supplies part of it).

▸ ASSURANCE flow (something breaks)

Problem Handling (CRM — what the customer sees) → Service Problem Mgmt (SM&O) → Resource Trouble Mgmt (RM&O — the actual fault).

▸ BILLING flow (usage → cash)

Resource Mediation & Reporting → Service Guiding & Mediation → Manage Billing Events + Charging → Bill Invoice Mgmt → Bill Payments & Receivables.

This is the "aha": map any telco activity to a Level-2 process, and you instantly know who owns it, what it hands to next, and which system (BSS or OSS) implements it. That's the literal skill assessed in eTOM Practitioner certification — As-Is vs To-Be mapping, gap analysis, RASCI.

Honest caveat — the live model has moved on: the clean 4×4 grid above is the canonical teaching model and still how most practitioners think. But the actual GB921 repository has been heavily restructured (v21.5 alone deleted 167 processes and added 540+, and removed the old "Common" domain; current release is v25.0, Nov 2025). Modern eTOM is more domain-based decomposition than rigid matrix, and — crucially — its Level-2/3 processes now trace directly to ODA functional blocks and components. So: learn the grid to think; use the live spec + ODA mapping to build.

How you actually use Level-2 (the practitioner's toolkit)

  • Scope a programme: circle the Level-2 boxes in play — instant, unambiguous project boundary everyone agrees on.
  • Gap analysis: map your As-Is systems onto the grid, then your To-Be; the deltas are your roadmap.
  • Kill duplication: if three systems all claim "Order Handling," the grid exposes it.
  • Write better RFPs: demand vendors state which Level-2 processes (and which Open APIs) each component covers.
  • Bridge to architecture: Level-2 process → ODA component → Open API → SID data entity. That chain is how a process map becomes a deployable system.

Charging & the OCS

Charging is the one BSS box where being wrong costs real money in real time, at millions of events per second, with no second chances. That's why it's the most contested, most specialised, most mission-critical system in the whole stack — and why it deserves its own room.

Explorer · the simple version

Two ways a shop can charge you. Postpaid = a bar tab: you drink all night, they total it up later (that's offline charging). Prepaid = an arcade card: before every single game the machine checks your balance and stops you the instant it's empty (that's online charging, run by the OCS). The OCS is a cashier so fast it can do that check millions of times a second and never make a mistake.

Offline vs Online — the fundamental split

DimensionOffline (OFCS)Online (OCS)
WhenAfter usage (post-paid)Before / during usage (pre-paid), real-time
Controls credit?No — just records what happenedYes — authorizes, reserves & can cut you off at zero
Classic usePostpaid contracts, enterprisePrepaid mobile, IoT, anything needing live control
4G interfaceDiameter GzDiameter Gy / Ro (and SS7/CAMEL for 2G/3G)
OutputCDRs → billing systemLive balance decrements + CDRs

Inside the OCS — the anatomy

Engine

Rating Function (RF)

Turns a raw event ("90 sec call", "50 MB") into a price using the tariff — the maths of money.

Wallet

Balance Mgmt (ABMF)

Account Balance Mgmt Function: holds & updates balances — money, free minutes, data buckets, loyalty points.

Trigger

CTF (in the network)

Charging Trigger Function: the network node (e.g. PGW/SMF) that fires a charging request whenever you use something.

Control

Credit-Control / Quota

The real-time loop that authorizes, grants quota, re-authorizes and finally debits — the part that must never blink.

The handshake that makes prepaid possible

This quota-reservation loop is the single most important flow in real-time charging. Master it and you understand the OCS.

OCS online charging — the credit-control / quota loop

Why "reserve, don't just debit": reserving a small quota up-front (not the whole balance) lets the OCS stay accurate even across many simultaneous sessions on the same balance, and cut a user off mid-session the moment credit runs out — without a round-trip per byte. That's the trick that made mass-market prepaid viable.

5G: the Converged Charging System (CCS) & CHF

3GPP collapsed the old split. In 5G's Service-Based Architecture, OCS and OFCS merge into one Converged Charging System, whose central entity is the CHF (Charging Function) — combining the online (OCF) and offline (CDF) roles, plus Rating (RF), Balance Mgmt (ABMF) and the Charging Gateway (CGF). The network talks to it over the service-based interface Nchf (JSON over HTTP/2), with backward-compatible Gy/Ro/Rf for 4G interworking.

Before 5G

Two siloed systems

Separate OCS (prepaid) + OFCS (postpaid) → duplicated tariffs, "spaghetti" rating, customers locked into prepaid or postpaid.

5G CCS

One convergent engine

Prepaid + postpaid + any service on one platform with shared balances — and the flexibility to charge per slice, per SLA, per event for B2B2X & IoT. This is the monetization unlock.

The concepts that make it powerful (and hard)

  • Monetary & non-monetary balances: not just rupees — free minutes, data buckets, loyalty points, all rated against in real time.
  • Shared & hierarchical balances: a family plan or an enterprise with 10,000 lines drawing on one pool — concurrency without double-spend.
  • Multi-dimensional tariffs: price by time-of-day, location, network, app, slice, QoS — all resolved live.
  • Policy vs charging: the PCF/PCRF decides what you're allowed (speed, priority); charging decides what it costs. They interplay constantly — e.g. throttle instead of cut off.
  • Session-based vs event-based: a data session (continuous) vs a single SMS or content purchase (discrete) — different charging operations.

Why charging is brutally hard — and the #1 vendor battleground: it must be simultaneously real-time (sub-millisecond), exact (it's money), carrier-grade highly-available (five-nines-plus), massively scalable (millions of TPS), and flexible enough for slicing/IoT/B2B2X. Very few products do all six. That's why charging vendors get acquired for premiums — see the

🇮🇳 India angle

India is overwhelmingly prepaid and operates at extraordinary scale (Jio alone serves hundreds of millions). That makes the OCS not a feature but the existential core of the business — real-time, exact, always-on, at volumes few markets ever test. Convergent charging is the strategic prize as operators chase postpaid, FWA, IoT and 5G B2B without rebuilding their rating estate.

The BSS vendor landscape

Two camps fight for the BSS estate: the incumbents who sell broad, battle-tested suites to Tier-1 operators, and the cloud-native / ODA-native challengers who promise speed and openness. Knowing who's who — and how 2025–26 reshuffled the board — is what lets you steer a build-vs-buy decision credibly.

Explorer · the simple version

Imagine buying the software to run a phone company. You can buy one giant, do-everything machine from a famous brand — powerful but heavy, pricey, and slow to change. Or you can assemble lighter "app-store" pieces from newer companies — faster and cheaper to change, but you have to fit them together. That's the whole fight.

Camp 1 — the incumbents (≈60% of the market)

Amdocs, Huawei, Ericsson, Nokia, Netcracker together hold roughly 60% of OSS/BSS revenue. They sell broad, end-to-end portfolios for large-scale, multi-year transformations at Tier-1 operators with messy legacy estates to untangle.

The well-understood trade-off: incumbents win on breadth and Tier-1 credibility, but tend toward high implementation cost, multi-year timelines, and release cycles that can't match the pace of commercial innovation. Strong at BSS depth; weak at speed and developer-first experience.

The 2025–26 consolidation wave (say this and you sound current)

In barely a quarter, the board was reshuffled — driven by AI + cloud pressure forcing sub-scale charging vendors to "merge or sink":

WhenDealWhy it matters

The pattern: standalone, best-of-breed charging is hard to sustain as a business — few mid-size players can afford the AI/cloud re-tooling. Expect more "absorb the specialist into a suite" moves. The incumbents are buying back the modularity story rather than being killed by it.

So how do you actually choose?

  • Greenfield / MVNO / digital sub-brand: go cloud-native / ODA-native. Speed and TCO win; you have no legacy to protect.
  • Tier-1 brownfield: rarely a big-bang rip-and-replace. The smart play is a strangler pattern — stand up ODA components alongside the monolith and migrate capability-by-capability, often with an incumbent's transformation programme. Hybrid is the norm.
  • Charging specifically: decide convergent-from-day-one; it's the hardest piece to swap later.
  • Always: insist on Open API conformance and (now) ODA component certification — that's what makes "mix-and-match" real rather than marketing.

The provocative, command-level take: while telco wrestled with charging standards, the cloud/AI industries built their own planet-scale, real-time metering & monetization without adopting telco BSS technology at all. The uncomfortable implication: if telco BSS stays slow, proprietary and monolithic, it risks growing irrelevance — out-engineered by hyperscaler billing.
